# Core Rules

## NW-001 — Write to communicate, not to impress.

People remember useful information, not impressive wording.

Bad:
> We are leveraging innovative strategies to drive transformational outcomes.

Better:
> We combined three approval steps into one process.

---

## NW-002 — Clarity over cleverness.

If a simpler sentence communicates the same idea, use it.

---

## NW-003 — Specificity over abstraction.

Bad:
> We improved efficiency.

Better:
> We reduced report generation from 18 minutes to 3.

---

## NW-004 — Evidence over enthusiasm.

Replace adjectives with facts.

Bad:
> Amazing customer experience.

Better:
> Support replies average 42 minutes during business hours.

# AI Writing Signals

## High Signal

### AI-001 — Repeated em dashes

One em dash is natural.

Multiple em dashes in nearly every paragraph create a recognizable rhythm.

### AI-002 — Predictable openings

Review:

- At its core...
- It is worth noting...
- In today's rapidly evolving...
- The key takeaway is...
- One of the biggest advantages...

Delete them if the sentence works without them.

### AI-003 — Empty transitions

Review:

- Furthermore
- Moreover
- Additionally
- Consequently
- Overall
- In conclusion

Keep only when they genuinely improve clarity.

### AI-004 — Constant positivity

Watch for:

- exciting
- amazing
- transformative
- powerful
- revolutionary
- game-changing

Replace praise with evidence.

### AI-005 — Symmetrical rhythm

Avoid four or five sentences that all have nearly identical length and structure.

## Medium Signal

- Three-item lists everywhere
- Constant summaries
- Bullet overload
- Repeated "however"
- Repeated "for example"

## Low Signal

These are poor indicators by themselves:

- Correct grammar
- Professional tone
- Oxford commas
- Complete sentences
- One em dash

# Mechanical Habits

Watch for:

- Every paragraph is the same size.
- Every sentence has similar length.
- Every section begins with a transition.
- Every article ends with a summary.
- Every YouTube script starts with "In today's video..."
- Every LinkedIn post starts with "I wasn't going to share this..."

Patterns matter more than words.

---

# Things Humans Naturally Do

- Vary sentence length.
- Occasionally interrupt themselves.
- Ask genuine questions.
- Start with observations.
- Leave transitions implied.
- Use occasional sentence fragments.
- Repeat naturally in speech.
- Qualify uncertainty when appropriate.

Examples:

"I think..."

"From what I've seen..."

"In my experience..."

"It depends."

---

# False Positives

## Em dashes

Not an AI signal by themselves.

The issue is overuse.

## Professional vocabulary

Words like API, compliance, depreciation, Kubernetes, amortization, audit, and containerization are appropriate when they improve precision.

## Formal tone

Formal writing is expected in many professional contexts.

The issue is unnecessary corporate language.

## Complete sentences

Clear writing is not an AI signal.

Never make writing worse just to appear more human.

# LinkedIn Pattern Library

The goal of LinkedIn writing is to communicate experience and useful observations—not to imitate motivational speaking or maximize engagement.

## LI-001 — "I wasn't going to share this..."

Avoid:
> I wasn't going to share this, but someone needs to hear it.

Better:
> Last week a client asked a question that changed how I think about hiring.

Why:
Artificial suspense delays the point.

---

## LI-002 — "Unpopular opinion..."

Avoid:
> Unpopular opinion: Leadership is about listening.

Better:
> I've found that the best project reviews start with questions instead of presentations.

Why:
Most "unpopular opinions" are actually common opinions.

---

## LI-003 — Story → Lesson → Moral

Template to avoid:

Problem → Emotional moment → Universal life lesson → "Thoughts?"

Better:

Event → Observation → What changed → Practical takeaway

---

## LI-004 — Engagement bait

Review endings like:

- Agree?
- Thoughts?
- Let that sink in.
- Read that again.
- Comment below.

Better:
Ask one specific question only if you genuinely want answers.

Example:

> How does your team decide when a project is ready to ship?

---

## LI-005 — Leadership clichés

Review:

- Leadership isn't about...
- Success is about...
- Great leaders always...
- Culture eats strategy...

Replace with a real event.

Example:

Instead of:
> Leadership is about trust.

Write:
> The project moved faster after every decision had one owner.

---

## LI-006 — Motivational inflation

Review words like:

- humbled
- honored
- beyond excited
- grateful beyond words
- life-changing
- dream come true

Use them only when they accurately reflect the situation.

---

## LinkedIn Editing Checklist

Before publishing ask:

- Did I start with a real observation?
- Did I remove generic inspiration?
- Did I replace abstract lessons with concrete examples?
- Would I actually say this out loud?
- Does every paragraph add new information?

# Corporate Editing Drill

When reviewing business writing, highlight every adjective and abstract noun.

For each one ask:

- Can I replace this with a fact?
- Can I replace this with a measurement?
- Can I replace this with a real action?
- Can I replace this with the actual people involved?

If the answer is yes, rewrite it.

# Editing Shortcut

When you see an adjective that praises the product, ask:

"What evidence would convince a skeptical reader?"

Replace the adjective with that evidence whenever possible.

# YouTube Pattern Library

The goal of a YouTube script is to sound spoken, specific, and paced like a real explanation.

## YT-001 — "In today's video..."

Avoid:
> In today's video, we're going to explore five powerful productivity strategies.

Better:
> I kept running into the same problem, so I tested five different approaches.

Rule:
Start with the reason the video exists, not the fact that a video is starting.

---

## YT-002 — "Stay until the end..."

Avoid:
> Stay until the end because the last tip is the best one.

Better:
> The last mistake is the one that cost me the most time.

Rule:
Create curiosity with substance, not a retention phrase.

---

## YT-003 — "Let's dive in."

Avoid as a default transition.

Better:
> Start with the first setting.

Rule:
If the phrase can be deleted, delete it.

---

## YT-004 — Generic curiosity bait

Avoid:
- You won't believe...
- This changes everything...
- The secret is...
- Nobody is talking about...

Better:
> I expected the expensive tool to win. It didn't.

Rule:
State the actual surprise.

---

## YT-005 — Spoken contractions

Prefer natural spoken phrasing when appropriate.

Instead of:
> You will notice...

Use:
> You'll notice...

Instead of:
> It does not work...

Use:
> It doesn't work...

Rule:
Scripts should sound like speech, not a blog post read aloud.

---

## YT-006 — Remove narrator language

Avoid:
- As previously mentioned...
- In this section...
- We will now examine...
- Let us explore...

Better:
Move directly into the next point.

---

## YouTube Editing Checklist

- Does the first 30 seconds contain a real reason to keep watching?
- Did I remove generic hype?
- Does the script sound natural when read aloud?
- Did I use examples instead of slogans?
- Does the ending give one clear takeaway or next step?

# Business Writing

Business writing should be precise, useful, and professional without sounding inflated.

## BW-001 — Name the decision

Avoid:
> We are evaluating strategic opportunities.

Better:
> We are deciding whether to expand into Canada next quarter.

---

## BW-002 — State the metric

Avoid:
> Performance improved.

Better:
> Average response time dropped from 11 hours to 3 hours.

---

## BW-003 — Replace conclusions with evidence

Avoid:
> The rollout was successful.

Better:
> 96% of users migrated without support tickets.

---

## BW-004 — Don't hide behind nouns

Avoid:
> Alignment, optimization, enablement, execution.

Better:
> Agree, improve, help, ship.

---

## BW-005 — Executive summaries

Weak:
> This document outlines several considerations related to the proposed launch.

Better:
> Recommendation: delay the launch two weeks to complete security testing.

Rule:
Start with the decision, recommendation, or risk.

---

## BW-006 — Technical precision

Good:
- OAuth token
- Kubernetes cluster
- EBITDA
- SOC 2
- API endpoint
- depreciation schedule

Rule:
Keep technical terms when they improve accuracy.

---

## Business Editing Checklist

- Can every claim be measured?
- Did I name the people or teams?
- Did I replace buzzwords with actions?
- Is every recommendation explicit?
- Would this make sense six months from now?

---

# Email Pattern Library

Email should reduce work for the reader.

## E-001 — Get to the point

Avoid:
> I hope this email finds you well.

Better:
> Quick question about Thursday's deployment.

---

## E-002 — One purpose

Every email should answer:
> What do I need from the reader?

If there are multiple requests, separate them clearly.

---

## E-003 — Clear requests

Weak:
> Let me know your thoughts.

Better:
> Can you approve this by Thursday at 3 p.m.?

---

## E-004 — Avoid over-softening

Weak:
> I just wanted to check...

Better:
> Do you have an update on the contract?

---

## E-005 — Better closings

Weak:
> Please let me know if you have any questions.

Better:
> If this timeline works, I'll send the final version tomorrow.

---

## E-006 — Subject lines

Weak:
> Update

Better:
> Budget Review — Approval Needed by Friday

---

## Email Editing Checklist

- First sentence explains the purpose.
- Requests include a date when needed.
- Filler greetings are removed unless warmth is needed.
- Unnecessary apologies are removed.
- The email ends with the next action.

---

# Blog Pattern Library

Blog writing should teach, not perform.

## B-001 — Skip dictionary introductions

Avoid:
> Productivity is the ability to...

Better:
> Most productivity problems start with unclear priorities.

---

## B-002 — Don't announce the article

Avoid:
> In this comprehensive guide, we'll explore...

Better:
> Here's what changed after we simplified the process.

---

## B-003 — Don't write for everyone

Avoid:
> Whether you're a founder, student, or marketer...

Better:
> This article is for managers running small software teams.

---

## B-004 — Replace broad claims

Avoid:
> AI is changing everything.

Better:
> AI reduced first-draft writing time, but editing still took the longest.

---

## B-005 — End with a useful implication

Avoid:
> The future is bright.

Better:
> Start by fixing one repeatable workflow before automating everything else.

---

## Blog Editing Checklist

- Does the first paragraph contain a real observation?
- Can every abstract claim become a concrete example?
- Did I remove generic transitions?
- Does the ending give the reader a next step?

---

# Social Media Pattern Library

Every platform rewards clarity. Do not confuse engagement tactics with good writing.

## SM-001 — Don't manufacture curiosity

Avoid:
> You won't believe what happened...

Better:
> I tested three approaches. One worked much better than the others.

---

## SM-002 — Don't force inspiration

Avoid:
> Never give up. Keep pushing.

Better:
> We solved the problem after removing one unnecessary approval step.

---

## SM-003 — Earn the hook

Weak:
> Here's a thread...

Better:
> The smallest change had the biggest impact.

Rule:
Hooks should come from a real fact, observation, or mistake.

---

## SM-004 — Avoid generic engagement bait

Review:
- Agree?
- Thoughts?
- Comment below.
- Read that again.
- Let that sink in.

Better:
Ask a specific question only when it adds value.

---

## SM-005 — Match the platform

X:
Short, direct, one idea.

Facebook:
More narrative and context.

Reddit:
Answer the question first. Avoid marketing language.

Instagram:
Caption supports the image instead of repeating it.

Threads:
Conversational. Do not recycle LinkedIn posts.

---

## Social Media Editing Checklist

- Is there a real observation in the first two lines?
- Did I remove hype?
- Did I replace buzzwords with specifics?
- Would I actually say this out loud?
- Is the post useful even if nobody comments?

# Corporate AI Speak Dictionary — Expansion 6: Management Speak

## C-041 — Bandwidth

Avoid:
> Do you have bandwidth to take this on?

Better:
> Do you have time to review this by Friday?

Rule:
Use “time,” “capacity,” or “availability” unless you are literally discussing system or network capacity.

---

## C-042 — Capacity

Avoid:
> We need to evaluate team capacity.

Better:
> The team can take on two more client projects this month.

Rule:
“Capacity” is useful only when tied to work, staffing, time, system load, or budget.

---

## C-043 — Ownership

Avoid:
> We need more ownership.

Better:
> Jordan owns the launch checklist and will update it every Friday.

Rule:
Name the owner and the responsibility.

---

## C-044 — Visibility

Avoid:
> We need more visibility into the process.

Better:
> We need to know which purchase orders are waiting for approval.

Rule:
Visibility into what?

---

## C-045 — Transparency

Avoid:
> We need more transparency.

Better:
> Send a weekly update with budget, risks, and unresolved decisions.

Rule:
Name the information that should be shared.

---

## C-046 — Accountability

Avoid:
> We need more accountability.

Better:
> Every task needs one owner and one due date.

Rule:
Turn accountability into responsibility, deadlines, and follow-through.

---

## C-047 — Priorities

Avoid:
> We need to align on priorities.

Better:
> We need to decide whether the billing fix or dashboard redesign ships first.

Rule:
Name the tradeoff.

---

## C-048 — Dependencies

Avoid:
> There are several dependencies.

Better:
> The launch depends on legal approval and the Salesforce export.

Rule:
Name what blocks the work.

---

## C-049 — Blockers

Avoid:
> We have blockers.

Better:
> Legal has not approved the vendor contract.

Rule:
Name the blocker and who can remove it.

---

## C-050 — Escalate

Avoid:
> We need to escalate this.

Better:
> We need Finance to decide today whether the budget is approved.

Rule:
Escalation should identify the person, decision, and deadline.

---

## C-051 — Governance

Avoid:
> We need stronger governance.

Better:
> Changes over $10,000 need approval from Finance and Legal.

Rule:
Replace governance with rules, roles, and approval paths.

---

## C-052 — Operating Model

Avoid:
> We need a new operating model.

Better:
> Support will handle tier-one issues, and Engineering will handle bugs confirmed by Support.

Rule:
Describe how the work will be divided.

---

## C-053 — Operating Cadence

Avoid:
> We need a better operating cadence.

Better:
> The team will meet Mondays to review risks and Fridays to review progress.

Rule:
State the meeting rhythm and purpose.

---

## C-054 — Execution

Avoid:
> We need better execution.

Better:
> We missed three deadlines because no one owned vendor approvals.

Rule:
Explain what failed.

---

## C-055 — Roadmap

Avoid:
> We need to align the roadmap.

Better:
> We need to decide whether reporting or billing improvements ship in Q3.

Rule:
Use “roadmap” only when referring to sequenced product or project work.

---

## C-056 — Strategic Priorities

Avoid:
> These are our strategic priorities.

Better:
> This quarter, we are reducing churn, shortening onboarding, and improving invoice accuracy.

Rule:
Name the actual priorities.

---

## C-057 — Resourcing

Avoid:
> We have a resourcing challenge.

Better:
> We need one more QA engineer for the migration project.

Rule:
Name the missing person, skill, budget, or tool.

---

## C-058 — Cross-Functional

Acceptable when accurate, but often overused.

Weak:
> We launched a cross-functional initiative.

Better:
> Product, Support, and Legal worked together to update the cancellation process.

Rule:
Name the teams.

---

## C-059 — Initiative

Avoid:
> We started a new initiative.

Better:
> We started calling customers 60 days before renewal.

Rule:
Call it a project, program, campaign, or action.

---

## C-060 — Moving Forward

Avoid:
> Moving forward, we will improve communication.

Better:
> Starting Monday, each team lead will send a Friday status update.

Rule:
Replace vague future language with a specific change.
